Educational attainment for marginalized students in the twenty-first century:

Examining trends over 20 years

By Alex Caban-Echevarria Published: August 16, 2023

College degrees have come to be a point of contention for people who either think they are hurting or helping entry-level workers. As a benchmark to most jobs in industries like business and media to education and social work, Bachelor’s degrees are becoming more common today.

For many, going to college at 18 years old is a right of passage. For some, a Bachelor’s degree has become the norm that “degree inflation” has barred them from pursuing a career they may already have the skills for.

The Education Trust, a project that puts together guides for lawmakers to “incorporate an explicit and targeted focus on opportunities and outcomes for students of color” measured that Black and Latinx student representation varies by state, and that each state should reduce race-based inequities.

Equity in education is important because regardless of where a student comes from, whether that be from generational wealth or marginalized communities, they have the right to excel and advance their education to increase their quality of life.

Higher levels of educational attainment cause stability, an article by Jennifer Karas Montez finds, as higher levels of education had bigger impacts on students with disadvantaged childhoods’ health and life expectancy, in comparison with adults who had advantaged childhoods.

The discussion around degree equity around the world has come to a head in the US. Before the affirmative action ruling by the Supreme Court, students were protected and offered more opportunities for scholarship and aid.

If jobs are requiring Bachelor’s or higher degrees, but students of color are not obtaining these degrees at the same rates as White students, then they will be less represented in jobs these jobs.
